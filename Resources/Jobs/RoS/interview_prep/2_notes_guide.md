# What You CAN Take In (Safely)

You can absolutely take:

### ✅ 1. Bullet-point prompts for behaviour stories

Not full scripts. Prompts.

Example:

**Changing & Improving – Legacy → Cloud**

* Situation: ageing on-prem PG cluster, patching pain, risk
* Task: modernise without outage
* Action: phased logical replication, IaC standardisation, monitoring uplift
* Result: 40% cost reduction, improved RTO, removed single point of failure

That’s perfect.

---

### ✅ 2. A generic case study structure template

This is powerful.

You could bring one page titled:

**Case Study Framework**

* Objectives
* Constraints
* Risks
* Options (min 2)
* Trade-offs (Cost / Risk / Debt / Operability)
* Recommendation
* Phased roadmap
* Governance & assurance

That ensures you don’t ramble under pressure.

This is not cheating. It’s professionalism.

---

### ✅ 3. NFR checklist

This is gold for this job.

Have a small checklist:

* Availability target?
* RTO / RPO?
* Security classification?
* Data residency?
* Performance SLAs?
* Monitoring / alerting?
* Backup strategy?
* Cost envelope?

If you systematically reference NFRs in the case study, you will look senior.

---

### ✅ 4. Key phrases aligned to Civil Service tone

Public sector loves language like:

* “Evidence-based options”
* “Proportionate governance”
* “Value for money”
* “Incremental modernisation”
* “Service continuity”
* “Risk mitigation”
* “Alignment to DDaT roadmap”

Having those phrases top-of-mind is smart.

# What You SHOULD NOT Do

🚫 Do not bring 10 pages of printed scripts
🚫 Do not read answers
🚫 Do not flip through folders mid-answer
🚫 Do not bring pre-written case study content

That looks insecure.

They want to see thinking ability.

---

# The Right Way To Use Notes

Subtle.

When they ask a behaviour question:

1. Pause.
2. Glance at bullet prompts.
3. Speak naturally.

If you maintain eye contact and speak confidently, notes won’t matter.

If you bury your head in paper, it kills authority.

---

# The Real Advantage

The biggest use of notes isn’t answers.

It’s control.

You can write at the top of your page:

**Slow down. Structure. Answer the question asked.**

Under pressure, senior candidates rush.

You don’t want to rush.

# Case Study Section

For the presentation, you can present from your written prep.

That’s completely normal.

In fact, structured handwritten diagrams can impress them more than slides.
